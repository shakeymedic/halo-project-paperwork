1. Executive Summary

Preliminary scoping (October 2025) identified a critical safety variance. While centralised equipment (e.g. the Central Line Trolley) was located in 91 seconds, dispersed HALO equipment (e.g. Thoracotomy kit) took over 5 minutes to retrieve. This project replicates the successful 'Central Line' storage model for all HALO procedures to bridge this safety gap. Using the Model for Improvement, we implement a dual-arm strategy. First, cognitive support via a Quick Reference Handbook (QRH) guide system. Second, infrastructure improvements by centralising dispersed equipment into a dedicated HALO Trolley.

2. Governance & Safety Standards (LOCSSIPs)

This project functions under the framework of NatSSIPs (National Safety Standards for Invasive Procedures). The HALO Trolley acts as the physical infrastructure to support the Departmental LOCSSIPs (Local Safety Standards).

  • Compliance: The QRH guides serve as the immediate safety checklist required by LOCSSIPs.
  • IPC: Trolley design and location vetted by the Infection Prevention Control Lead to ensure cleaning compliance.
  • Oversight: Discrepancies in stock will be reported via Datix to the Patient Safety Team.

Ethics & Registration: This project is classified as a Quality Improvement initiative in accordance with the NHS Health Research Authority (HRA) decision tool and does not constitute research. No patient-identifiable data is collected at any stage. All timing data is anonymised at source and linked only to staff role. Staff participation in the "Stopwatch Audit" and knowledge assessments is voluntary. No formal ethical approval from a Research Ethics Committee (REC) is required. The project has been registered with the Trust Clinical Audit & Effectiveness Department in accordance with local governance requirements.

3. Patient & Public Involvement (PPI)

Addresses the patient safety priority: "Right Kit, Right Place, Right Time". Delays in HALO scenarios are directly linked to mortality.

  • Patient Safety Partner (PSP): The project governance and risk assessment have been reviewed by the Trust Patient Safety Group.
  • Second Victim: This project mitigates the psychological impact on staff by reducing cognitive load during high-stress crises.

4. Problem & Background

HALO procedures require immediate action. Current equipment storage is fragmented across multiple stores. This increases cognitive friction. A pilot audit (N=3) demonstrated that finding "scattered" stock takes >300 seconds (5 mins), whereas "trolley-based" stock takes just 91 seconds. The infrastructure directly dictates retrieval speed, regardless of clinician seniority.

4b. Evidence Base

The concept of cognitive offloading through standardised equipment organisation is well-established in high-reliability industries. In healthcare, the "Resuscitation Trolley" model reduces time-to-intervention in cardiac arrest scenarios. The Royal College of Emergency Medicine (RCEM) Quality Standards for Major Trauma (2024) emphasise the importance of immediately accessible procedural equipment. The National Confidential Enquiry into Patient Outcome and Death (NCEPOD) repeatedly highlights equipment availability as a contributory factor in suboptimal care during emergency procedures. The QRH system draws on principles from aviation checklist design and the NHS Emergency Department Checklist programme.

6. Methodology & Measurement Strategy (SQUIRE 2.0)

We utilise the Model for Improvement. To address "Work as Done" vs "Work as Imagined", baseline scoping includes a qualitative questionnaire. A pre-intervention and post-intervention knowledge assessment of procedural skills provides objective evidence of the QRH's educational impact on clinician preparedness.

Type Measure Description Rationale & Data Plan
Outcome "Time to Kit" (Seconds) Method: "Stopwatch Audit" (In-situ sim).
Control: Timer starts at 'Scenario Declaration' (e.g. "Traumatic Arrest") to capture cognitive load and mitigate Hawthorne effect.
Goal: Reduce mean time by 50%.
Outcome Procedural Knowledge (Pre/Post) Method: Structured knowledge assessment administered before and after QRH deployment.
Analysis: Wilcoxon signed-rank test to assess statistical significance of score changes.
Goal: Demonstrate statistically significant improvement in procedural knowledge scores.
Process Checklist Compliance % Method: Weekly audit of the "Broken Tag Log".

Balancing Stock Cost / Theft Method: Procurement review.
Goal: <£100 loss per month.
Qualitative Staff Confidence Method: Likert Scale Survey (Pre/Post).

7. Baseline Data: Run Chart Analysis

PILOT SCOPING (PROOF OF CONCEPT)
We compared "Time to Kit" for existing centralised stock vs dispersed stock:
  • Control (Central Line Trolley): 91 Seconds (Safe)
  • Test (Thoracotomy, Dispersed): >5 Minutes (Unsafe)
Conclusion: Centralising equipment reduces retrieval time by >60%.

In accordance with RCEM best practice, data is displayed as a Run Chart to identify special cause variation. Baseline data combines Pilot and Phase 1 timings for statistical stability (Total N=13). A minimum of 10 data points is recommended by the Institute for Healthcare Improvement (IHI) for reliable run chart interpretation.

Run Chart Interpretation Rules (IHI): Non-random variation is identified by several rules. Rule 1 is a Shift: 6 or more consecutive points all above or below the median. Rule 2 is a Trend: 5 or more consecutive points all increasing or decreasing. Rule 3 involves assessing against the expected runs table. Rule 4 relates to an Astronomical data point. If any rule is met, there is less than a 5% probability this pattern occurred by chance alone. Post-intervention, we will look specifically for a shift below the baseline median to confirm the trolley intervention has produced a sustained improvement in retrieval times.

Study: Identify redundant items causing clutter.
Act: Create a proposal for bespoke, streamlined ED procedural trays.

13. Implementation Playbook (The 'How-To')

1. The Matron Pitch (Budget & Safety)

When asking for approval, do not frame this as "new equipment". Frame it as "Risk Reduction".

"While there is a small initial setup cost for the frame, this project generates long-term savings. Centralising stock reduces our waste costs significantly while bridging a critical safety gap."
2. Restocking Protocol (The Tag System)

Nursing Lead: Sarah Hart, Senior Sister.

Trigger: Use of HALO Trolley is rare. Restock is not routine. It is ad-hoc.

  • 1. Clinical Staff: Break the tag and use the kit. The team that uses the kit is responsible for replacing the drawer contents immediately after the case.
  • 2. Integrity Check: Twice daily at handover. Visual check of tags only. No need to count individual items if the tag is intact.
  • 3. If Broken: Shift Coordinator alerts Stores Team if items are missing.
3. Junior Leadership Protocol

How to manage the SHO Lead (Dr Maida):

  • Portfolio: Explicitly offer a QIP participation assessment upon completion.

14b. Limitations

This project has several acknowledged limitations which should be considered when interpreting the findings:

Limitation Description Mitigation
Single-site design Findings may not be directly generalisable to other departments with different layouts or case-mix. The underlying principle is generalisable even if specific timings are not. The project methodology is designed to be replicable.
Small sample size Baseline data is N=13. The sample is too small for parametric statistical testing of timing data. Run chart methodology is specifically designed for small-sample QI work and does not require large N.
Hawthorne effect Staff who know they are being timed may perform differently to their normal behaviour. Mitigated by using a scenario-based approach where the timer starts at scenario declaration. This captures the cognitive processing time.

15. RCEM Portfolio Reflection (Project Lead/Trainee)

This section meets the requirements for the FRCEM/Portfolio "Quality Improvement" domain.

PERSONAL REFLECTION: LEADERSHIP & CHANGE

1. What was the most difficult part of this project?
"Moving from 'Work as Imagined' to 'Work as Done'. I assumed staff knew where kit was. The baseline data showed huge variation. Convincing senior nursing staff to allow a new trolley required negotiation skills and a focus on Patient Safety. Securing multi-stakeholder buy-in required tailored communication for each group's priorities."

2. What did I learn about the Model for Improvement?
"That PDSA cycles must be small. We reordered our cycles to start with the cognitive aid booklet (PDSA 1) before introducing the physical trolley (PDSA 2). The data collection using a stopwatch provided objective evidence to support the change. Adding a knowledge assessment gave us a reliable outcome measure."

3. What did I learn about leadership and delegation?
"Effective QI requires delegation with clear accountability. By assigning Dr Maida (SHO) the data collection and Jemma (EDP) the nursing dissemination, I created a team with defined roles. This also provided portfolio evidence for the junior team members."

4. Sustainability Plan:
"To ensure this does not fail when I rotate, we agreed a formal tagging process with Senior Sister Sarah Hart. The team that uses the kit replaces the drawer contents. The QRH guides have been peer-reviewed by the consultant body for long-term clinical credibility."
