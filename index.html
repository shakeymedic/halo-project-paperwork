Improving Readiness for High Acuity Low Occurrence (HALO) Procedures

Project Lead: Dr Jake Turner Department: Emergency Medicine
Start Date: October 2025 Planned Review: June 2026

1. Executive Summary

This Quality Improvement Project (QIP) targets the critical safety gap in equipment accessibility for High Acuity, Low Occurrence (HALO) procedures. October 2025 baseline data indicates 72.9% of staff lack confidence in locating life-saving equipment. Using the Model for Improvement, this project implements a dual-arm strategy: (1) Infrastructure: Centralising dispersed equipment into a dedicated HALO Trolley, and (2) Cognitive Support: Deploying a "Two-Page" guide system. To ensure sustainable change and data rigour, leadership of the "Time-to-Kit" audit workstream has been delegated to the junior medical team (ST3/SHO).

2. Ethics & Governance

Classified as Service Improvement; measures performance against RCEM/Resus Council UK standards. No formal ethical approval required. Registered as a departmental service evaluation.

3. Patient & Public Involvement (PPI)

Addresses the patient safety priority: "Right Kit, Right Place, Right Time." Delays in HALO scenarios (e.g., CICO) are directly linked to mortality. This project also mitigates the "Second Victim" effect by reducing staff cognitive load during crises.

4. Problem & Background

HALO procedures require immediate action. Current equipment storage is fragmented (Ophthalmology store, Obs/Gynae box, Resus store), increasing cognitive friction. Baseline analysis confirms this "scattered" infrastructure is the primary driver of low clinician confidence.

12. Implementation Playbook (The 'How-To')

1. The Matron Pitch (Budget & Safety)

When asking for approval, do not frame this as "new equipment". Frame it as "Risk Reduction".

"Currently, we have all this expensive kit (Gigli saws, Rapid Rhinos) scattered in cupboards where it expires unnoticed. By centralising it in one trolley, we actually save money on expired stock and ensure it's used correctly. This is a zero-cost intervention using existing stock lines, but it mitigates a high-level SI risk."
2. Fidelity Protocol (Stopping Theft)

The biggest risk to this QIP is staff "borrowing" scissors or rapid rhinos for non-emergencies.

  • The Tag System: Use plastic "breakaway" tags (like on the defib) for the drawers.
  • The Rule: If the tag is broken, the checklist must be signed.
  • The Culture: "If you break the seal, you own the restock."
3. Junior Leadership Protocol

How to manage the ST3/SHO Lead:

  • Define the Output: "I need 10 timings by Friday."
  • Weekly 5-Min Feedback: Meet every Friday. Look at the data sheets together.
  • Portfolio: Explicitly tell them: "If you complete this data collection, I will sign you off for a QIP participation assessment."

Appendix 17: Flash Teaching Script (5 Mins)

To be delivered at Handovers (08:00 / 16:00) during Week 5.

LEAD: "Team, 3 minutes on the new HALO Trolley."

1. THE WHY: "We found that 70% of us couldn't find kit for rare emergencies (e.g. cutting a neck or chest). This delay kills."

2. THE WHAT: "We have built the HALO Trolley. It lives in Resus Bay 1. It contains EVERYTHING for: Canthotomy, Hysterotomy, Thoracotomy, Escharotomy, and MaxFax bleeds."

3. THE HOW: "Look at the guides attached to the handle. Page 1 is the 'Kit Dump' (what to open). Page 2 is the 'Action Card' (what to do)."

4. THE ASK: "Please don't scavenge from it for minor cases. If you use it, tell the coordinator so we can restock. Go and open a drawer today to see the layout."

END.
