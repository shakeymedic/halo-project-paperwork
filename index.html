Improving Readiness for High Acuity Low Occurrence (HALO) Procedures

Project Lead: Dr Jake Turner Department: Emergency Medicine
Start Date: October 2025 Planned Review: June 2026

1. Executive Summary

This Quality Improvement Project (QIP) addresses critical variation in staff confidence and equipment awareness for High Acuity, Low Occurrence (HALO) procedures within the Emergency Department. Baseline data (October 2025) revealed that 72.9% of staff could not confidently locate equipment for life-saving interventions such as lateral canthotomy or resuscitative hysterotomy. This report details PDSA Cycle 1: the implementation of a dedicated HALO Trolley paired with the Departmental HALO Procedures Master 2025 "Two-Page System" guides, aiming to standardize cognitive support and reduce risk.

2. Ethics & Governance

This project is classified as a Service Improvement / Quality Improvement initiative (not research) as it measures performance against established standards (RCEM, DAS, Resus Council UK) to improve local care delivery. Formal ethical approval was not required. The project is currently being conducted as a local departmental service evaluation and will be registered with the audit department retrospectively if required.

3. Patient & Public Involvement (PPI)

While direct patient involvement in HALO procedures is limited due to their emergent nature, this project addresses the patient safety priority of "Right Kit, Right Place, Right Time." Delays in locating equipment for time-critical procedures (e.g., CICO, thoracotomy) directly impact mortality. Furthermore, providing cognitive aids reduces the "Second Victim" effect on staff, ensuring a more resilient workforce for future patient care.

4. Problem & Background

HALO procedures demand immediate, error-free execution. However, cognitive load theory suggests that during high-stress events, the capacity for "search tasks" (finding kit) competes with clinical decision-making. Our department currently stores equipment in dispersed locations (Ophthalmology store, Obs/Gynae box, Resus store), creating unnecessary friction. Baseline analysis confirms that this "scattered" infrastructure correlates directly with low clinician confidence. Note on Scope: While the baseline survey focused on 6 core procedures, the implemented intervention (HALO Trolley & Guide) addresses the full spectrum of critical interventions, including Pericardiocentesis, as per standard major trauma guidelines.

9. Stakeholder Engagement

Successful change requires multidisciplinary buy-in. Key engagement steps included:

  • Resus Nursing Team: Consultation on trolley layout to ensure it fits physical workflows and existing checking routines.
  • Consultant Body: Agreement on the clinical content of the HALO guides (aligned with DAS/RCEM guidelines).
  • Pharmacy: Agreements established for restocking non-standard items (e.g., Danazol, specific sealants) to prevent stockouts.

11. PDSA Cycle 1: Implementation

Objective: Test if centralising equipment and implementing the new cognitive aids improves staff confidence.

Plan

  • Design "HALO Trolley" distinct from the standard arrest trolley.
  • Finalise Departmental HALO Procedures Master 2025 (aligned with RCEM/DAS).
  • Create "Two-Page System": Page A (Kit Dump/Walkthrough) and Page B (Action/Flowchart).

Do (Current Phase)

  • Trolley: Procured, labelled, and placed in Resus (Nov 2025).
  • Guides: Printed, laminated, and attached via retractable lanyard.
  • Digital Access: Online Digital Guide QR codes added to trolley.
  • Education: "Handover Tours" commenced (Dr Turner leading).

Study (Planned)

  • Re-Survey: Scheduled for Feb 2026 (3 months post-implementation).
  • Audit: Review of weekly checklist compliance to be conducted Jan 2026.

Act (Planned)

  • Adaptation: Feedback on guide clarity will be incorporated into v2.
  • Expansion: If successful, concept will be expanded to Paediatric Resus.

13. Project Lead Reflection

Dr Jake Turner: "Leading this QIP has highlighted the distinct difference between 'availability' and 'accessibility'. While we technically possessed the equipment for these procedures, the cognitive friction required to assemble it rendered it effectively unavailable during a crisis. Engaging the nursing team early was pivotal; their insights on trolley 'real estate' prevented us from creating a system that would be rejected by daily users. Moving forward, the challenge will be maintaining the 'fidelity' of the trolley (preventing item migration) which is why the Process Measure of checklist compliance is so vital."

14. Appendix: Trolley & Drawer Configuration

Following consultation with the Resus Nursing team, the trolley has been condensed to a 6-Drawer Stack. To accommodate key procedures without waste, "Internal Dividers" have been used for drawers containing low-volume kit (e.g. Canthotomy and Pericardiocentesis). Safety Note: Airway/FONA equipment remains in the dedicated Difficult Airway Trolley to avoid duplication and fragmentation.
