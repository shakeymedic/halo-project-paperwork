Improving Readiness for High Acuity Low Occurrence (HALO) Procedures

Project Lead: Dr Jake Turner Department: Emergency Medicine
Start Date: October 2025 Planned Review: June 2026

1. Executive Summary

Preliminary scoping (October 2025) identified a critical safety variance. While centralised equipment (e.g., the Central Line Trolley) was located in 91 seconds, dispersed HALO equipment (e.g., Thoracotomy kit) took over 5 minutes to retrieve. This project replicates the successful 'Central Line' storage model for all HALO procedures to bridge this safety gap. Using the Model for Improvement, we implement a dual-arm strategy: (1) Infrastructure: Centralising dispersed equipment into a dedicated HALO Trolley, and (2) Cognitive Support: Deploying a "Two-Page" guide system.

2. Governance & Safety Standards (LOCSSIPs)

This project functions under the framework of NatSSIPs (National Safety Standards for Invasive Procedures). The HALO Trolley acts as the physical infrastructure to support the Departmental LOCSSIPs (Local Safety Standards).

  • Compliance: The "Two-Page" guides serve as the immediate safety checklist required by LOCSSIPs.
  • IPC: Trolley design and location to be vetted by the Infection Prevention Control Lead (via Sarah Hayward) to ensure cleaning compliance.
  • Oversight: Discrepancies in stock (e.g., missing difficult airway kit) will be reported via Datix to the Patient Safety Team.

3. Patient & Public Involvement (PPI)

Addresses the patient safety priority: "Right Kit, Right Place, Right Time." Delays in HALO scenarios are directly linked to mortality.

  • Patient Safety Partner (PSP): The project governance and risk assessment have been reviewed by the Trust Patient Safety Group (Lay Member included).
  • Second Victim: This project mitigates the psychological impact on staff by reducing cognitive load during high-stress crises.

4. Problem & Background

HALO procedures require immediate action. Current equipment storage is fragmented (Ophthalmology store, Obs/Gynae box, Resus store), increasing cognitive friction. A pilot audit (N=3) demonstrated that finding "scattered" stock takes >300 seconds (5 mins), whereas "trolley-based" stock takes just 91 seconds. The infrastructure directly dictates retrieval speed, regardless of clinician seniority.

13. Implementation Playbook (The 'How-To')

1. The Matron Pitch (Budget & Safety)

When asking for approval, do not frame this as "new equipment". Frame it as "Risk Reduction".

"We ran a timing test. It currently takes over 5 minutes to locate a chest opening kit because it is scattered. In contrast, it takes just 90 seconds to locate a central line because it is in a dedicated trolley. We are not asking for new equipment. We are simply asking to move existing HALO stock into the same format as the Central Line kit to save 3.5 minutes of critical time when a patient is dying."
2. Fidelity Protocol (Stopping Theft)

The biggest risk to this QIP is staff "borrowing" scissors or rapid rhinos for non-emergencies.

  • The Tag System: Use plastic "breakaway" tags (like on the defib) for the drawers.
  • The Rule: If the tag is broken, the checklist must be signed.
  • The Culture: "If you break the seal, you own the restock."
3. Restocking Protocol (The "Pull" System)

Primary Contacts: Khan Zaman / Lisa / Sue (Stores).

Trigger: Use of HALO Trolley is a "Never Event" in daily practice. Restock is not routine; it is ad-hoc.

  • 1. Clinical Staff: Break tag, use kit, leave trolley "dirty."
  • 2. Shift Coordinator (Band 7): Checks trolley at 08:00. If tag broken:
    • Logs Datix.
    • Places "Restock Request" card in Khan Zaman's pigeonhole (or specific email to Stores Team).
  • 3. Stores Team: Refill specific drawer and apply new tag.
4. Junior Leadership Protocol

How to manage the ST3/SHO Lead:

  • Define the Output: "I need 10 timings by Friday."
  • Weekly 5-Min Feedback: Meet every Friday. Look at the data sheets together.
  • Portfolio: Explicitly tell them: "If you complete this data collection, I will sign you off for a QIP participation assessment."
5. The "Soft Power" Strategy (Support Services)

How to handle the non-clinical stakeholders who can make or break the project:

The Domestics (Cleaners):
"Hi [Name], this trolley contains equipment for emergency surgery. We have taped a red box on the floor for it. Please can you ensure it stays inside this box? If you need to clean behind it, please ask the Charge Nurse to move it for you, as it has power cables for the bone drill that must stay plugged in."
The Patient Safety / Governance Team:
"I am helping you close a gap in our NatSSIPs compliance. Currently, we have no checklist for these procedures. By approving this trolley and the attached guides, we are creating a 'forcing function' that ensures the checklist is at the patient's bedside. Can we agree to log this project as a formal risk control measure?"

15. RCEM Portfolio Reflection (Project Lead/Trainee)

This section meets the requirements for the FRCEM/Portfolio "Quality Improvement" domain.

PERSONAL REFLECTION: LEADERSHIP & CHANGE

1. What was the most difficult part of this project?
"Moving from 'Work as Imagined' to 'Work as Done'. I assumed staff knew where kit was. The baseline data (Run Chart) showed huge variation. Convincing senior nursing staff to allow a new trolley required negotiation skills and a focus on 'Patient Safety' rather than just 'New Kit'."

2. What did I learn about the Model for Improvement?
"That PDSA cycles must be small. We initially wanted to restock the whole Resus room. We scaled this down to just 'One Trolley' (PDSA 1) to prove the concept before expanding. The data collection using a stopwatch provided objective evidence to support the change."

3. Sustainability Plan:
"To ensure this doesn't fail when I rotate, I have embedded the 'Checklist Compliance' into the Band 7 daily workflow and appointed a permanent 'HALO Guardian' from the nursing faculty."
