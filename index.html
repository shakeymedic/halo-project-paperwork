1. Executive Summary

A baseline survey of ED clinical staff at Birmingham Heartlands ED (October–November 2025, N=16) confirmed a critical readiness gap for High Acuity Low Occurrence (HALO) procedures: no respondent (0/16) could correctly identify the equipment location for all 6 HALO procedures, and only 3 of 16 (18.8%) reported being able to at least "assist with guidance" for every procedure. 14 of 16 (87.5%) said a dedicated HALO trolley would be "very useful". An accompanying scoping audit confirmed that centralised equipment (the Central Line Trolley) was located in under 2 minutes, while dispersed HALO equipment (thoracotomy kit) took over 5 minutes — confirming that infrastructure, not seniority, dictated retrieval speed.

Using the Model for Improvement, this project deploys a dual-arm intervention strategy. PDSA Cycle 1 (Cognitive Infrastructure) provides a peer-reviewed Quick Reference Handbook (QRH) for 8 HALO procedures using a standardised two-page format. PDSA Cycle 2 (Physical Infrastructure) consolidates dispersed equipment into a dedicated HALO Trolley with a visual-check tagging system. PDSA Cycle 3 (Kit Rationalisation) streamlines theatre packs to remove redundant items and embeds the QRH inside each pack.

Current project status (May 2026): Cycle 1 has progressed through Plan, Do and Study (a structured peer-review questionnaire of consultants and senior trainees, N=10, closed 15 May 2026 with mean Likert ratings of 4.6–5.0 across all domains). Equipment-list revisions arising from peer review have been agreed; the QRH is being printed and laminated for standalone deployment in Resus during May–June 2026. Cycles 2 and 3 are sequenced to follow.

2. Governance & Safety Standards (LOCSSIPs)

This project functions under the guidelines of NatSSIPs (National Safety Standards for Invasive Procedures). The HALO Trolley acts as the physical infrastructure to support the Departmental LOCSSIPs (Local Safety Standards).

  • Compliance: The QRH guides serve as the immediate safety checklist required by LOCSSIPs.
  • IPC: Trolley design and location vetted by the Infection Prevention Control Lead to ensure cleaning compliance.
  • Oversight: Discrepancies in stock will be reported via Datix to the Patient Safety Team.

Ethics & Registration: This project is classified as a Quality Improvement initiative in accordance with the NHS Health Research Authority (HRA) decision tool and does not constitute research or formal audit. No patient-identifiable data is collected at any stage. All timing data is anonymised at source and linked only to staff role. Staff participation in the "Stopwatch Audit" and knowledge assessments is voluntary. No formal ethical approval from a Research Ethics Committee (REC) is required. The project has been shared with the departmental governance lead and is being presented at the departmental governance meeting as part of the dissemination plan.

3. Patient & Public Involvement (PPI)

This project addresses the patient safety priority "Right Kit, Right Place, Right Time". Delays in HALO scenarios are directly linked to mortality.

  • Patient Safety Group: The project will be presented at the departmental governance meeting as part of its dissemination plan. Where appropriate, the Trust Patient Safety Group will be engaged. This is an honest acknowledgement that formal Patient Safety Partner review has not yet occurred — it is part of the planned governance pathway.
  • Second Victim: This project mitigates the psychological impact on staff by reducing cognitive load during high-stress crises. A clinician supported by structured cognitive aids and immediately available kit can preserve their judgement bandwidth for clinical decision-making.
  • Outcome alignment: "Time to Kit" is a direct surrogate for "Time to Intervention" in the most time-critical procedures in Emergency Medicine.

Honest limitation: this is a system-level intervention, not a co-designed patient-facing service change. A future iteration could include a brief patient information sheet for survivors of HALO procedures who recover capacity — this is on the project sustainability backlog.

4. Problem & Background

HALO procedures share three features that combine into a clear patient safety problem: each is life-saving, each is performed rarely (so skill and system memory both decay), and each requires immediate access to specialised equipment that is not in the standard arrest trolley. Current equipment storage is fragmented across the Ophthalmology store (lateral canthotomy), Obstetrics box (hysterotomy), Resus store cupboards (thoracotomy, chest drain) and the standard arrest trolley (limited maxillofacial kit).

A structured baseline survey (October–November 2025, N=16; 7 HST4+ trainees, 4 SHOs, 4 ACPs, 1 Other) found:

  • 0/16 (0%) of staff could correctly identify the equipment location for all 6 HALO procedures. Pooled across procedure-responses, only 24% knew the correct location.
  • 0/16 (0%) of staff reported being confident to perform all 6 procedures independently. Only 3/16 (18.8%) could at least "assist with guidance" for every procedure.
  • 14/16 (87.5%) of staff said a dedicated HALO trolley with printed aide-memoires would be "very useful"; 12/16 (75%) said an ALS-style flowchart would be "absolutely" useful.

An accompanying scoping audit demonstrated that finding "scattered" stock takes over 5 minutes, while "trolley-based" stock takes under 2 minutes. The infrastructure directly dictates retrieval speed, regardless of clinician seniority.

4b. Evidence Base

The concept of cognitive offloading through standardised equipment organisation is well-established in high-reliability industries. In healthcare, the "Resuscitation Trolley" model reduces time-to-intervention in cardiac arrest scenarios. The Royal College of Emergency Medicine (RCEM) Quality Standards for Major Trauma (2024) emphasise the importance of immediately accessible procedural equipment. The National Confidential Enquiry into Patient Outcome and Death (NCEPOD) repeatedly highlights equipment availability as a contributory factor in suboptimal care during emergency procedures. The QRH system draws on principles from aviation checklist design and the NHS Emergency Department Checklist programme. The intervention design draws explicitly on the Hierarchy of Intervention Effectiveness: cognitive aids (the QRH) are mid-tier (memory aid), while the HALO trolley is high-tier (forcing function and standardisation). Deploying both gives a defence-in-depth approach.

5. SMART Aims

By June 2026:

  • Outcome 1 — Knowledge: Increase the proportion of ED clinical staff who can correctly identify the equipment location for all 6 HALO procedures from 0% (baseline October 2025, N=16) to ≥75%.
  • Outcome 2 — Confidence: Increase the proportion of ED clinical staff who report at least "assist / perform with guidance" for all 6 HALO procedures from 18.8% (baseline) to ≥75%.
  • Process — Efficiency: Reduce mean "Time to Kit" for 6 core HALO procedures by 50% from the consolidated baseline.
  • Process — Compliance: Maintain ≥95% weekly trolley integrity-check compliance once the trolley is live.
  • Balancing: Zero incidents of stock depletion in donor areas (Ophthalmology, Obstetrics, Resus) attributable to the HALO Trolley restock policy.

Note on target choice: 75% (rather than 90%) was set after reviewing the baseline distribution. Even staff identifying as "Senior middle grade HST4+" had heterogeneous knowledge and confidence. A 75% target is realistic and clinically meaningful, while accepting that some procedures (e.g. resuscitative hysterotomy) will always have a long tail of clinicians who never expect to perform them independently.

Run Chart Interpretation Rules (IHI): Non-random variation is identified by several rules. Rule 1 is a Shift: 6 or more consecutive points all above or below the median. Rule 2 is a Trend: 5 or more consecutive points all increasing or decreasing. Rule 3 involves assessing against the expected runs table. Rule 4 relates to an Astronomical data point. If any rule is met, there is less than a 5% probability this pattern occurred by chance alone. Post-intervention, we will look specifically for a shift below the baseline median to confirm the trolley intervention has produced a sustained improvement in retrieval times.

14b. Limitations

This project has several acknowledged limitations which should be considered when interpreting the findings:

Limitation Description Mitigation
Single-site design Findings may not be directly generalisable to other departments with different layouts or case-mix. The underlying principle is generalisable even if specific timings are not. The project methodology is designed to be replicable.
Baseline survey composition The baseline survey (N=16) included no consultants. Composition: 7 HST4+ trainees, 4 SHOs, 4 ACPs, 1 Other. The senior consultant view of equipment location knowledge is therefore not captured in baseline data. Consultant input was captured separately through the QRH peer review questionnaire (N=10, of which 3 consultants). Re-survey at 3 months post-deployment will deliberately target consultant participation.
Baseline timing data — small pilot The "Time to Kit" baseline currently rests on a small pilot (approximately 5–6 timed retrievals). This is too small to plot a robust pre-intervention median. Baseline timing data is being consolidated before PDSA Cycle 2 launch. Run chart interpretation will be deferred until at least 12 baseline data points are accumulated, in line with IHI guidance.
Hawthorne effect Staff who know they are being timed may perform differently to their normal behaviour. Mitigated by using a scenario-based approach where the timer starts at scenario declaration. This captures the cognitive processing time.
QRH feedback sample QRH feedback collected from N=10 senior clinicians; nursing and resident doctor (SHO) voices are under-represented in the formal survey. Cycles 2 and 3 will include nursing and SHO feedback channels via the tag system audit and post-intervention stopwatch debriefs.
Self-selection and ceiling effects in QRH feedback Respondents may be those most engaged with HALO procedures; ceiling effects (mean ≥4.6 across all domains; overall recommendation 10/10) may reflect respondent enthusiasm as well as instrument quality. Triangulate with objective "Time to Kit" data and post-deployment knowledge assessment in Cycles 2 and 3.
Project status at submission At the point of this ARCP submission, PDSA Cycle 1 has progressed through Plan, Do and Study and is currently in Act. Cycles 2 and 3 are sequenced to follow. Full post-intervention outcome data is therefore not yet available. The project will continue beyond this ARCP. 15. ST6 Reflection: Leadership, Learning & Longitudinal QI Development

This reflection meets the RCEM 2021 Curriculum (2025 Update) requirement for ST6 to demonstrate mature reflection on a trainee-led project and to summarise the longitudinal development of QI and leadership skills across the entirety of EM training (SLO11).

15.1 Personal qualities and self-awareness

What enabled this project to improve patient care. The single most useful trait I drew on was the willingness to interrogate the gap between availability and accessibility. The equipment existed. It had always existed. The system around the equipment was the problem. Recognising this earned me the right to redesign the system rather than just ask for more kit.

Deliberate restraint. The instinct in a QI project is to do everything at once — the cognitive aid, the trolley, the kit revision, the simulation training. I held back. Sequencing the three PDSA cycles separately means each one's effect can be measured cleanly. This was a methodological discipline rather than a personal preference, and it cost me momentum at times.

Weaknesses I noticed. I am inclined to over-engineer. The first draft of the QRH was a 50-page handbook. It took peer feedback (and my own irritation at re-reading my own draft) to recognise that a clinician in a crisis needs two pages, not fifty. I am also prone to building the technical artefact (the QRH, the trolley specification) before doing the political work (the engagement). The political work is harder and slower, and I default to the comfortable task first. Recognising this tendency has been one of the genuine pieces of self-knowledge I take from this project, and I have actively pushed myself to put the conversations ahead of the spreadsheets.

Working under pressure. The project ran alongside full clinical duties, a partner expecting our first child in late June 2026, and ST6-level departmental responsibilities. I learnt to use small windows of time efficiently — drafting the QRH in 30-minute blocks rather than waiting for an afternoon I never got. The trade-off was that some weeks the project moved slowly. This is a realistic feature of QI work in training, and I think the panel will recognise the genuine challenge of pursuing a multi-month intervention in parallel with clinical training.

Seeking feedback. The QRH peer review questionnaire was deliberately structured to ask for feedback in a form that respondents could give honestly without spending much time on it. The 18-item Likert format with optional free text generated 10 responses in three weeks. The structure made it easy to spot the one consultant whose detailed free-text feedback (pledgets, cord clamps) was the highest-value input in the entire dataset.

15.2 Mapping to the EM Leaders framework

The behaviours I have most actively developed during this project map to the following EM Leaders / NHS Leadership Academy framework domains:

  • Demonstrating personal qualities — self-management, integrity, and ongoing self-development through the iterative refinement of the QRH and project plan.
  • Working with others — building relationships across the medical, nursing, operational and procurement teams; choosing 1:1 differentiated engagement over committee work.
  • Managing services — managing performance through measurement (run charts, peer review), and managing resources (the trolley quote, stock relocation logistics).
  • Improving services — encouraging improvement and innovation; applying knowledge and evidence (Model for Improvement, IHI run chart rules, Hierarchy of Intervention Effectiveness); managing change incrementally through staged PDSA cycles.

The domain I am still developing is Setting Direction at the departmental level. I led this project; I have not yet led the strategic improvement programme of the department as a whole. That is appropriately a consultant-level activity, and I expect to develop it through my first consultant post.

During ST4 and ST5 I engaged in QI activity that did not result in formal QIATs but shaped how I approach this project:

  • Attempted Learning Disability Patient Passport for the ED. I designed a single-page passport for patients with learning disabilities to make their reasonable adjustments visible at every interaction. The project failed at implementation: it depended on nursing staff adding paper-based effort to an already pressured workflow, and the EPR could not be updated to host the passport electronically within the project timeline. The learning from that failure was specific and applied directly to the HALO project:
    1. never design an intervention that adds paper work to a nursing workflow with no offsetting reduction elsewhere — find the cognitive aid that replaces rather than adds work;
    2. confirm the technology pathway before designing the workflow, not after;
    3. get nursing in the room during design, not during deployment.
    Each of those lessons is visible in how the HALO project has been structured — particularly the co-designed tag system rather than an additive paper checklist.
  • Educational quality improvement through the WMEBEM platform. I have developed and deployed multiple clinically-grounded educational tools through the West Midlands Evidence Based Emergency Medicine platform, including a VBG/ABG interpreter, an acute frailty pathway, a procedural sedation record form, paediatric rashes guidance, a TST/MITT triage trainer, and a high-fidelity scenario simulator. These sit within the SLO11 definition of QI activity that improves training and educational quality. Where direct user feedback has shaped iterative improvements (e.g. the VBG/ABG interpreter was migrated to a different AI backend after evaluation), the iterative method has been the same as the PDSA approach used in the HALO project.
  • Belmont infuser clinical reference document (ST5). A short-cycle improvement to embed a reliable cognitive aid for the use of a critical resuscitation device, with attention to print compatibility — the same design principle applied to the HALO QRH.

How my QI thinking has developed across training. At earlier stages, my projects were essentially audit-and-improve — measure a thing, push for compliance with a standard, re-audit. The Learning Disability passport was the moment I learnt the limits of that approach: technically clean audits can hide socially or operationally unimplementable interventions. The HALO project is the first one in which I designed with stakeholder constraints at the centre rather than at the periphery. The methodological diversity (driver diagram, fishbone, Hierarchy of Intervention Effectiveness, peer review survey, run charts) reflects an attempt to match tools to problem features rather than applying a single template to every situation.

15.4 What I will carry into consultant practice
  • The single most important QI lesson from training is: engage the people who do the work, in the design phase. Everything else is downstream of this.
  • Failed projects are more instructive than successful ones, provided the failure is examined honestly. The Learning Disability passport gave me more methodological discipline than any of my successful projects.
  • Sequence interventions to make the measurement interpretable, even if it slows the work. A noisy run chart is worse than a slightly slower implementation.
  • Do the political work first; the technical work is the comfortable bit.
  • I expect to take on QI supervision and coaching of trainees in my first consultant post, and I see this as a natural progression from leading SHO and EDP team members through this project.
15.5 Sustainability after I leave the department

The project has been designed for sustainability after I rotate. The tagging system, co-designed with Senior Sister Sarah Hart, places responsibility for restock on the team that breaks a tag — so the workflow does not depend on a named project lead. The QRH has been peer-reviewed by the consultant body so its clinical credibility is collectively owned rather than tied to my authorship. The trolley quote and stock relocation plan are documented in the Implementation Playbook and Master Stocking Order so that procurement and stores have the operational knowledge to maintain it. The dissemination plan (Section 14c) sets out how the project will continue to be reported on after my departure.
