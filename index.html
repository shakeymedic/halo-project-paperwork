1. Executive Summary

Preliminary scoping (October 2025) identified a critical safety variance. While centralised equipment (e.g., the Central Line Trolley) was located in 91 seconds, dispersed HALO equipment (e.g., Thoracotomy kit) took over 5 minutes to retrieve. This project replicates the successful 'Central Line' storage model for all HALO procedures to bridge this safety gap. Using the Model for Improvement, we implement a dual-arm strategy: (1) Infrastructure: Centralising dispersed equipment into a dedicated HALO Trolley, and (2) Cognitive Support: Deploying a "Two-Page" Quick Reference Handbook (QRH) guide system.

2. Governance & Safety Standards (LOCSSIPs)

This project functions under the framework of NatSSIPs (National Safety Standards for Invasive Procedures). The HALO Trolley acts as the physical infrastructure to support the Departmental LOCSSIPs (Local Safety Standards).

  • Compliance: The "Two-Page" QRH guides serve as the immediate safety checklist required by LOCSSIPs.
  • IPC: Trolley design and location to be vetted by the Infection Prevention Control Lead (via Sarah Hayward) to ensure cleaning compliance.
  • Oversight: Discrepancies in stock (e.g., missing HALO trolley equipment) will be reported via Datix to the Patient Safety Team.

Ethics & Registration: This project is classified as a Quality Improvement initiative in accordance with the NHS Health Research Authority (HRA) decision tool and does not constitute research. No patient-identifiable data is collected at any stage; all timing data is anonymised at source and linked only to staff role (not individual). Staff participation in the "Stopwatch Audit" and knowledge assessments is voluntary. No formal ethical approval from a Research Ethics Committee (REC) is required. The project has been registered with the Trust Clinical Audit & Effectiveness Department in accordance with local governance requirements (Registration No: [Insert]).

3. Patient & Public Involvement (PPI)

Addresses the patient safety priority: "Right Kit, Right Place, Right Time." Delays in HALO scenarios are directly linked to mortality.

  • Patient Safety Partner (PSP): The project governance and risk assessment have been reviewed by the Trust Patient Safety Group (Lay Member included).
  • Second Victim: This project mitigates the psychological impact on staff by reducing cognitive load during high-stress crises.

4. Problem & Background

HALO procedures require immediate action. Current equipment storage is fragmented (Ophthalmology store, Obs/Gynae box, Resus store), increasing cognitive friction. A pilot audit (N=3) demonstrated that finding "scattered" stock takes >300 seconds (5 mins), whereas "trolley-based" stock takes just 91 seconds. The infrastructure directly dictates retrieval speed, regardless of clinician seniority.

4b. Evidence Base

The concept of cognitive offloading through standardised equipment organisation is well-established in high-reliability industries. In healthcare, the "Resuscitation Trolley" model has been shown to reduce time-to-intervention in cardiac arrest scenarios. The Royal College of Emergency Medicine (RCEM) Quality Standards for Major Trauma (2024) emphasise the importance of immediately accessible procedural equipment. The National Confidential Enquiry into Patient Outcome and Death (NCEPOD) has repeatedly highlighted equipment availability as a contributory factor in suboptimal care during emergency procedures. The "Two-Page" QRH system draws on principles from aviation checklist design and the NHS Emergency Department Checklist programme, where structured cognitive aids reduce error rates during high-acuity, low-frequency events.

In accordance with RCEM best practice, data is displayed as a Run Chart to identify special cause variation. Baseline data combines Pilot (N=3) and Phase 1 (N=10) timings for statistical stability (Total N=13). A minimum of 10 data points is recommended by the Institute for Healthcare Improvement (IHI) for reliable run chart interpretation; N=13 exceeds this threshold and provides a stable baseline median.

Run Chart Interpretation Rules (IHI): Non-random variation is identified by: (1) Shift ‚Äî 6 or more consecutive points all above or below the median; (2) Trend ‚Äî 5 or more consecutive points all increasing or decreasing; (3) Too few or too many runs ‚Äî assessed against the expected runs table for a given number of data points; (4) Astronomical data point ‚Äî a data point that is clearly different from all others, agreed by those close to the process. If any rule is met, there is less than a 5% probability this pattern occurred by chance alone. Post-intervention, we will look specifically for a shift below the baseline median to confirm the trolley intervention has produced a sustained improvement in retrieval times.

13. Implementation Playbook (The 'How-To')

1. The Matron Pitch (Budget & Safety)

When asking for approval, do not frame this as "new equipment". Frame it as "Risk Reduction".

"While there is a small initial setup friction (sourcing the frame), this project generates long-term savings. Currently, we expire multiple sets of rarely used kit across three different cupboards. Centralizing them reduces our 'waste-on-expiry' costs significantly, while bridging a critical safety gap."
2. Fidelity Protocol (Stopping Theft)

The biggest risk to this QIP is staff "borrowing" scissors or rapid rhinos for non-emergencies.

  • The Tag System: Use plastic "breakaway" tags (like on the defib) for the drawers.
  • The Rule: If the tag is broken, the checklist must be signed.
  • The Culture: "If you break the seal, you own the restock."
3. Restocking Protocol (The "Pull" System)

Primary Contacts: Khan Zaman / Lisa / Sue (Stores). Nursing Lead: Jemma (EDP).

Trigger: Use of HALO Trolley is a "Never Event" in daily practice. Restock is not routine; it is ad-hoc.

  • 1. Clinical Staff: Break tag, use kit, leave trolley "dirty."
  • 2. Integrity Check: Twice daily (08:00 & 20:00) at handover. Visual check of tags only.
  • 3. If Broken: Shift Coordinator logs Datix and alerts Stores Team for refill.
4. Junior Leadership Protocol

How to manage the SHO Lead (Dr Maida):

  • Weekly 5-Min Feedback: Meet every Friday. Look at the data sheets together.

14b. Limitations

This project has several acknowledged limitations which should be considered when interpreting the findings:

Limitation Description Mitigation
Single-site design This QIP is conducted at a single Emergency Department (Birmingham Heartlands Hospital). Findings may not be directly generalisable to other departments with different layouts, staffing models, or case-mix. The underlying principle (centralised equipment reduces retrieval time) is generalisable even if specific timings are not. The project methodology is designed to be replicable.
Small sample size Baseline data is N=13 and post-intervention data will be approximately N=10. While sufficient for run chart analysis per IHI guidance (‚â•10 data points), the sample is too small for parametric statistical testing of timing data. Run chart methodology is specifically designed for small-sample QI work and does not require large N. The pre/post knowledge assessment will use an appropriate non-parametric test (e.g., Wilcoxon signed-rank) if sample size is insufficient for parametric analysis.
Hawthorne effect Staff who know they are being timed may perform differently to their normal behaviour, potentially underestimating true baseline retrieval times. Mitigated by using a scenario-based approach ("Stopwatch Audit") where the timer starts at scenario declaration, not when the individual begins searching. This captures the cognitive processing time and more closely reflects real clinical use.
Simulated vs. real clinical use Timing data is collected during in-situ simulation, not during actual patient care. Real emergencies involve additional stressors, competing tasks, and time pressure not fully replicated in simulation. The "Hot Debrief" trigger card (Appendix 6) is designed to capture real-world use data as a secondary source. In-situ simulation in the actual clinical environment provides higher ecological validity than laboratory simulation.
Confounding variables Improvements in retrieval time may be partially attributable to staff becoming more familiar with the department layout over time (independent of the trolley intervention), particularly during a rotation period. By deliberately including staff at various stages of their rotation in both pre- and post-intervention samples, this effect is partially controlled. The magnitude of expected improvement (>50%) makes it unlikely that familiarity alone accounts for the change.
Sustainability uncertainty Long-term compliance with the checking and restocking process is unproven at the time of writing. Equipment pilferage and tag compliance are theoretical risks. Quarterly audit schedule (Section 14), induction integration, and appointment of a permanent "HALO Guardian" (Jemma, EDP) are designed to mitigate this. Sustainability will be assessed at 6 and 12 months.

PERSONAL REFLECTION: LEADERSHIP & CHANGE

1. What was the most difficult part of this project?
"Moving from 'Work as Imagined' to 'Work as Done'. I assumed staff knew where kit was. The baseline data (Run Chart) showed huge variation. Convincing senior nursing staff to allow a new trolley required negotiation skills and a focus on 'Patient Safety' rather than just 'New Kit'. "That PDSA cycles must be small. We initially wanted to restock the whole Resus room. We scaled this down to just 'One Trolley' (PDSA 1) to prove the concept before expanding. The data collection using a stopwatch provided objective evidence to support the change. Adding a pre/post knowledge assessment (led by Dr Maida) gave us a second robust outcome measure beyond the timing data alone."

"Effective QI requires delegation with clear accountability. By assigning Dr Maida (SHO) the knowledge assessment and data collection, and Jemma (EDP) the nursing dissemination and checking process, I created a team with defined roles rather than attempting to do everything myself. This also provided portfolio evidence for the junior team members."
